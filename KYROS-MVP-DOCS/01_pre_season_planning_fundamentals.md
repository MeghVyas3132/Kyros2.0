# 01 — Pre-Season Planning: Fundamentals

## The One-Line Definition

Pre-season planning is **capital allocation under uncertainty, with irreversibility**.

A merchandising VP commits cash 4–9 months before the consumer signal exists. The commitment cannot be unwound. The only lever after commitment is markdown — which costs 30–50% of margin.

Every other definition of pre-season planning (assortment, buying, range planning, OTB) is an operational sub-activity of this core bet.

---

## Why Pre-Season Planning Exists

### 1. Lead time is structural
Apparel lead time from fabric sourcing → cut/sew → QC → warehouse → store shelf is **4–6 months** for woven, **3–4 months** for knits, 8–10 for specialized fabrics. The buy decision must be made before the season's weather, wedding calendar, competitor launches, and macro conditions are known.

### 2. Fabric and vendor economics force early commit
- Fabric is dyed in lots; lots have MOQs of thousands of meters
- Vendors allocate factory capacity 3–6 months ahead; late POs lose slots
- Advances of 20–30% are paid at PO; another 30–40% on fabric-in

By the time the season's first week of sell-through happens, **60–70% of season capital is already committed**.

### 3. Demand is fundamentally unforecastable from first principles
Fashion demand is driven by:
- Micro-trends (often 6–10 weeks from trend emergence to mass adoption)
- Weather realized vs forecast
- Competitor pricing and launch timing
- Macro sentiment (festival spend, discretionary income)
- Influencer and editorial hits

None of these are knowable at plan time. Planning is a **bet on probability distributions**, not a forecast.

### 4. If you don't plan, you over-buy by reflex
Buyers, without a disciplined plan, default to "last year + 10%" or to vendor-driven catalogs. Both under-weight capital discipline. The purpose of a plan is not to predict perfectly — it is to **constrain discretion**.

---

## The Three Risks Pre-Season Planning Manages

### Risk 1 — Quantity Risk
How many total units should enter the season?
- Overbuy: inventory piles up, working capital is tied, end-of-season markdown destroys margin
- Underbuy: stockouts erode sales, customer trust, and brand perception

A 10% over-buy on a ₹100Cr buy plan at 45% margin becomes a 20–30% earnings hit after markdown clearance.

### Risk 2 — Mix Risk
Across what categories, price bands, stories, and styles should units be distributed?
- Wrong mix is more insidious than wrong quantity — you can have perfect total units with totally wrong allocation across product
- Wrong price-band mix erodes full-price sell-through
- Wrong category mix (over-bought dresses, under-bought bottoms) kills cross-category basket size

### Risk 3 — Distribution Risk
Once goods arrive, where do they go?
- Right inventory at wrong stores = stockout in A+ stores, markdown in C stores
- The math difference between "fair share" allocation and demand-weighted allocation is typically **8–15% of achievable sellthrough**

These three risks compound. A plan that gets quantity right but mix wrong is almost as damaging as a plan that misses both.

---

## Irreversibility — What Cannot Be Undone

Once the following happen, they are effectively locked:

| Event | Reversibility | Cost to Reverse |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| PO placed with advance paid | Low | Lose advance (20–30% of PO value) |
| Fabric cut | None | 100% of fabric cost |
| Garments in warehouse | None | Only markdown clears |
| Allocation to stores | Partial | Transfer orders cost 3–5% in logistics + damage |
| Store opening marked in plan | Partial | Delay reduces allocation share |

The implication: pre-season decisions must carry the weight of **permanence**. Post-hoc corrections are ~always more expensive than getting the plan right.

---

## Blast Radius of Wrong Decisions

Concrete numbers on a ₹100Cr-buy brand (~₹180–220Cr retail):

### Over-buy by 15%
- ₹15Cr excess inventory
- ~60% clears at full price, ~40% needs markdown
- Markdown depth averages 35%
- Direct margin hit: ₹15Cr × 40% × 35% = **~₹2.1Cr margin destroyed**
- Plus: working capital locked for 6 months, lost optionality on next season's buy

### Under-buy 5% on hero styles
- ~25–30% of revenue comes from the top 10–15% of styles
- A 5% shortfall in hero depth translates to 10–15% sellthrough loss on those styles
- Lost revenue: ~₹2–3Cr at full price, ~₹1–1.5Cr margin forgone
- Intangible: empty shelves in A+ stores erode brand premium

### Wrong store mix
- Top 15% of stores typically do 35–40% of category revenue
- If allocation gives them only 20% of hero styles (under-concentration), you get stockouts where demand exists and overstocks where it doesn't
- Net: ~8–12% sellthrough degradation, or ~₹1.5–2Cr

### Wrong size mix
- Size curve misfit (e.g. one-size-fits-all instead of store-specific)
- In India: southern stores over-index S/M, northern stores over-index L/XL
- Typical waste: 8–15% of units end up in the wrong store's size buckets, requiring transfer or markdown

**Aggregate: a typical ₹100Cr brand with sloppy pre-season planning loses ₹5–8Cr of realizable margin every season** compared to a disciplined planner. Most of that loss is invisible — it manifests as "markdown was heavier than expected" rather than "the plan was wrong."

## The Irreducible Hardness

Even a perfect plan has residual error because:

- Consumer demand has genuine stochasticity
- Weather, macro, and competitor moves are exogenous
- Store-level signal is noisy at low ROS (1–2 units/week)

The goal of planning is **not zero error** — it is:

1. **Quantified error** (knowing the variance, not just the mean)
2. **Asymmetric error** (tilted toward recoverable mistakes, not terminal ones)
3. **Learning error** (feeding residuals back to improve next season)

A plan that loses ₹3Cr with a clear hypothesis about what went wrong is more valuable than a plan that loses ₹1.5Cr but cannot explain why.

---

## What This Means For KYROS

The product must:

1. **Force discipline** — constrain the buyer's discretion with explicit budget and depth limits
2. **Model the bet, not just record it** — show the distribution of likely outcomes, not just a point estimate
3. **Make irreversibility visible** — flag the moment when cash commitment becomes unwindable
4. **Trace every decision** — so that post-season residuals can be mapped back to specific assumptions
5. **Default to auditability over sophistication** — a VP who cannot explain the recommendation to their CEO will not use the tool

A merchandising VP does not want an AI oracle. They want a **decision system whose reasoning they can own**.

That framing drives everything downstream.
